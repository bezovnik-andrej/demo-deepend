Executive Summary

Norveo presents a coherent, domain-aware tool for pool professionals — but three systemic experience risks show up before users reach the product’s strengths.

First, progress is not preserved across sessions: long configuration work can be lost when the browser tab closes or the session ends. For people who work in the field and get interrupted often, that is the highest-risk gap in the journey.

Second, inclusive design is incomplete: labels and fields are not always clearly paired for assistive technology; icon-only navigation and custom controls lack clear names and selection states; keyboard and screen reader users are not on equal footing with mouse users.

Third, misleading affordances: many controls look active (Save, Export PDF, Templates, Share, Settings, context-menu “Add to plan” actions) but do not produce visible outcomes. That pattern trains users to doubt the whole interface.

The foundation is strong: grouped wizard steps, conditional visibility, template pre-fill, and a clear engineering narrative. Closing the gaps above is mostly about aligning design intent with behavior — clearer states, honest disabled or “coming soon” treatments, and consistent inclusive patterns.

Methodology & Scope

Heuristic evaluation against Nielsen’s ten usability principles, supplemented by WCAG-oriented accessibility review (perceivable, operable, understandable, robust) and task-based walkthroughs of the live experience — aligned with design specs, UI library documentation, and journey maps.

Surfaces reviewed

  • Full-screen configuration flow (template choice through final review)
  • Application shell: top bar, activity rail, tool strip, status bar, workspace tabs
  • All configuration steps and shared form patterns
  • Design workspace and drawing canvas
  • Engineering, Estimate, Bill of Materials, and Summary workspaces
  • Back-office administration (mock)

Evaluation lenses

  • Primary journeys — happy path from starting a project through reviewing engineering outputs
  • Edge & recovery — empty content, errors, partial completion, interrupted sessions
  • Heuristic pass — Nielsen’s ten heuristics
  • Accessibility — keyboard paths, screen reader compatibility, contrast and motion (WCAG 2.2 AA as reference)
  • Visual & language consistency — patterns, terminology, hierarchy, and theme behavior

SEO & discovery

Search and sharing considerations for a complex desktop-style application — relevant when marketing pages, help, or public demos ship.

Unique titles and descriptions. Each major entry point (portal, workspace areas, documentation) should carry a distinct page title and meta description when indexed or shared, so links in search and social previews read clearly.

Stable, human-readable URLs. Hash-heavy routing limits how much search engines can treat as separate landing experiences; marketing and help content benefits from canonical URLs and structured landing pages for high-intent queries (for example pool quoting, pool engineering).

Structured content. Public-facing docs and case studies should use a clear heading hierarchy (one main topic per page, logical subheads) and descriptive link text — good for both SEO and assistive technology.

This audit document itself uses a single descriptive title, meta description, and semantic headings to remain readable when shared or archived.

Misleading or inactive controls

Controls that look actionable but do not produce a clear outcome — they erode trust across the product.

NF-1 Save, Templates, Share, and Settings show no outcome Blocker
Surface
Top title bar — primary global actions
Evidence
Tapping Save, Templates, Share, or Settings does not open a flow, show confirmation, or change state. The keyboard save shortcut also gives no feedback — people cannot tell whether work is stored.
Impact
Everyone who expects to save or share hits a dead end; trust in the whole chrome drops.
Recommendation
Either ship the behavior or show disabled styling with short copy (“Coming soon” / “Not available in this preview”). Save should always acknowledge the gesture — even a lightweight “Saved” or “Save not available yet.”
NF-2 Export PDF in Engineering has no visible result Blocker
Surface
Engineering workspace — toolbar
Evidence
Export PDF reads as the way to hand off calculations; nothing happens after click.
Impact
Blocks the natural “share with client” moment after reviewing engineering data.
Recommendation
Deliver a PDF or print view, or clearly mark the control as future and offer an interim (e.g. print stylesheet).
NF-3 Export and Save on Estimate read as primary but do nothing Major
Surface
Estimate workspace — toolbar
Evidence
Save is visually emphasized; the grid looks editable like a spreadsheet but cells are not editable.
Impact
People expect a financial save path and a spreadsheet they can change — both are unclear.
Recommendation
Align visuals with capability: if not editable, soften the spreadsheet metaphor and disable or relabel Save/Export until they work.
NF-4 Canvas “Add to plan” menu closes without adding Major
Surface
Design canvas — context menu
Evidence
Choosing an equipment type dismisses the menu with no placement or preview on the drawing.
Impact
Core design task (adding equipment) feels broken on first try.
Recommendation
Hide the menu until placement works, or show a clear “Preview / placement” step and feedback.
NF-5 Bill of Materials — Export and Order Parts lack outcomes Minor
Surface
Bill of Materials workspace — toolbar
Evidence
Order Parts is visually primary; taps do not start an order or export flow.
Impact
Lower than global Save, but still weakens trust in the BOM workspace.
Recommendation
Disable or relabel until flows exist; keep primary styling for the first real action only.

Accessibility & inclusive design

Gaps aligned with WCAG-style expectations: perceivable information, operable controls, understandable relationships, robust support for assistive tech.

A-1 Form labels and fields are not clearly paired Blocker
Surface
All project configuration steps — text fields
Evidence
Labels sit beside fields visually but are not reliably associated for assistive technology; required fields show an asterisk without a matching programmatic “required” signal.
Impact
Screen reader users cannot confidently know what each field is for; tap-to-focus from label may not work.
Recommendation
Pair every label with its control in the design system; document the pattern for designers and copy “required” where needed.
A-2 Option lists and multi-selects do not announce selection state Blocker
Surface
Wizard — most choice steps
Evidence
Controls look like radio groups and checklists but behave like generic buttons; selection state is only visual.
Impact
Assistive tech cannot convey which option is chosen; keyboard users lack expected arrow-key patterns.
Recommendation
Specify in the design system: single-select vs multi-select, focus order, and announced state for each pattern.
A-3 Activity rail icons are unnamed in assistive tech Major
Surface
Left activity rail — Objects, Configure, Components, AI
Evidence
Tooltips are hidden from assistive tech; only icons are exposed.
Impact
Primary navigation is unusable for many screen reader users.
Recommendation
Give each control a concise accessible name matching the visible tooltip.
A-4 Configuration overlay does not trap focus Major
Surface
Full-height configuration drawer over the workspace
Evidence
Keyboard focus can move behind the overlay; closing does not return focus predictably; close control needs a clear name.
Impact
Keyboard and screen reader users lose orientation and may activate hidden content.
Recommendation
Match the modal pattern used elsewhere: keep focus inside until closed, restore focus to the opener, label the overlay and close.
A-5 Workspace tabs are not exposed as a tab pattern Major
Surface
Bottom workspace switcher (Design, Summary, Engineering, …)
Evidence
Looks like tabs but does not follow tab keyboard conventions; layer tabs in the title bar share the issue.
Impact
Keyboard-only users cannot use arrow keys between tabs as expected.
Recommendation
Define tab semantics and keyboard behavior in the pattern library; apply consistently.
A-6 Status toggles and zoom do not expose on/off or action names Major
Surface
Status bar — Grid, Snap, Dims, zoom; theme control in title bar
Evidence
On/off state is only visual; zoom buttons lack spoken names.
Impact
Screen readers hear “button” without whether Grid or Snap is active.
Recommendation
Expose pressed state and short labels for every icon-only control.
A-7 AI chat updates are not announced Major
Surface
AI Assistant panel — message stream and input
Evidence
New assistant messages do not surface as a live region; input placeholder alone is not a reliable name.
Impact
People using screen readers may miss replies.
Recommendation
Design for polite announcements of new messages and a clear input label.
A-8 Page regions are not easy to skim by landmark Minor
Surface
Overall application shell
Evidence
Chrome, canvas, and tabs are not clearly marked as header, main content, navigation, and status.
Impact
Screen reader users cannot jump quickly between major regions.
Recommendation
Map regions in the layout spec: header, main, nav, footer/status.
A-9 Drawing canvas is not described; add menu is pointer-only Minor
Surface
Design canvas — drawing and context menu
Evidence
The plan graphic lacks a short description; add flows rely on right-click.
Impact
Blind users get little context; keyboard users cannot mirror the add flow.
Recommendation
Add a plain-language summary of the drawing and a keyboard path to the same actions.

Configuration flow & content

The project setup journey — structure, validation, and clarity of progress.

W-1 Session ends — long flows are not recovered Blocker
Surface
Multi-step configuration wizard
Evidence
Closing the tab or browser loses in-progress answers; only lightweight preferences persist.
Impact
High for field workers interrupted mid-flow.
Recommendation
Design for resume: autosave, “Continue where you left off,” and clear messaging if session is lost.
W-2 Continue and Skip do not differ in rigor Major
Surface
Wizard footer — Continue, Skip step
Evidence
Both advance without enforcing required answers; required markers are mostly visual.
Impact
People can enter the workspace with empty critical fields.
Recommendation
Continue should validate required fields; Skip should explicitly mark steps as deferred.
W-3 Repeated steps show the same screen Major
Surface
Local code and mechanical groups — sub-steps
Evidence
Multiple dots in a group can open the same layout people just completed.
Impact
Confusion (“Didn’t I already fill this?”) and doubt about the wizard.
Recommendation
Merge into one expandable step or design distinct content per sub-step.
W-4 “None” can combine with real options Major
Surface
Heating and water features — multi-select
Evidence
None can coexist with other choices — logically inconsistent.
Impact
Downstream specs may receive impossible combinations.
Recommendation
Design mutual exclusion: choosing None clears others; choosing an option clears None.
W-5 Review step never reads as complete Major
Surface
Final review — stepper and summary table
Evidence
Completion indicator never turns “done”; long values are truncated in the table.
Impact
People doubt whether everything is ready; cannot verify full addresses.
Recommendation
Align completion rules with the rest of the journey; allow full text or expand-on-demand in review.
W-6 No focus move into new step content Minor
Surface
Wizard — step changes
Evidence
After Continue, focus stays on the button instead of the new question.
Impact
Extra tabbing for keyboard users across many steps.
Recommendation
Move focus to the step heading or first field — specify in the flow spec.
W-7 Sub-step dots are tiny and weakly labeled Minor
Surface
Wizard — dots under group circles
Evidence
Small hit targets; only hover title for names.
Impact
Hard to discover and operate for many users and assistive tech.
Recommendation
Increase target size, add visible labels or a simpler navigation pattern.

Trust, feedback & truth in numbers

When the system is silent, or numbers look live but are not.

D-1 Finalize can lock or unlock without confirmation Major
Surface
Title bar — finalize / lock
Evidence
Single action toggles lock; edits after lock may vanish without explanation.
Impact
Accidental lock or silent rejection of edits.
Recommendation
Confirm lock/unlock; when locked, show a persistent banner and disabled fields.
D-2 Summary and engineering figures can contradict the project Major
Surface
Summary and Engineering workspaces
Evidence
Some headline numbers stay fixed regardless of configuration; labels like “calculated” can appear on static figures.
Impact
Wrong sizing or pricing decisions.
Recommendation
Bind numbers to real inputs or badge them clearly as sample / illustrative.
D-3 Status bar coordinates and zoom feel disconnected Minor
Surface
Status bar — coordinates and zoom
Evidence
Coordinates read as live but do not track; zoom may not match canvas scale.
Impact
Looks broken to precision users.
Recommendation
Sync to real pointer and zoom, or remove until the behavior is real.

Visual design & content quality

Theme, typography, and copy polish.

V-1 Light theme is uneven — logo, canvas, chrome Minor
Surface
Global — logo, drawing canvas, status bar, tabs
Evidence
Some colors stay fixed while the rest of the UI switches; contrast on canvas may suffer in light mode.
Impact
Brand and readability feel inconsistent in light theme.
Recommendation
Define light-theme tokens for logo, canvas strokes, and chrome in the design system.
V-2 AI messages show raw formatting markers Minor
Surface
AI Assistant — message content
Evidence
Bold markers and line breaks appear as plain characters instead of rich text.
Impact
Assistant feels unfinished.
Recommendation
Render simple rich text or strip markers in the content spec.

Navigation & discovery

Finding the next project and understanding what applies where.

N-1 No obvious “new project” from the workspace Minor
Surface
Main workspace — global chrome
Evidence
Starting another project is not visible in the shell; back-office entry points may show placeholder messaging.
Impact
People must know to return to the hub.
Recommendation
Put “New project” in the title area or file menu; document in onboarding.
N-2 Canvas tools visible outside Design Observation
Surface
Status bar — grid, snap, dimensions
Evidence
Same controls appear on non-design workspaces where they do not apply.
Impact
Minor clutter and confusion.
Recommendation
Show canvas tools only in Design or dim them elsewhere.

Strengths

What already works well — these are foundations to build on, not just "the parts that aren't broken."

  • Wizard step grouping and conditional visibility — The 6 groups follow a logical progression (Project → Code → Design → Mechanical → Finishes → Review). Conditional steps like Tile Details and Code Details only appear when relevant, reducing cognitive load for simple projects.
  • Template pre-filling with badge indicators — The "Pre-filled by template" badge clearly communicates which steps already have values, reducing perceived effort and giving users a head start.
  • Back-navigation preserves data — Moving backward through wizard steps does not clear previously entered values. This is a frequently-overlooked detail that prevents frustration.
  • Domain-rich engineering workspace — The Engineering workspace demonstrates real domain expertise: turnover rate calculations, pipe sizing tables with velocity indicators, and clear differentiation between code-minimum, calculated, and user-adjustable values.
  • Bill of Materials part-swap pattern — The part swap flow uses a focused modal with clear title and purpose — a stronger pattern than some other overlays; it should be the reference for future modal work.
  • Clean visual design language — Dark tool-inspired chrome, consistent type scale, and cohesive tokens create a professional, focused workspace. The design system’s three-tier type scale is clearly defined.
  • Dual configuration entry points — Full-screen setup for first-time configuration and a side panel for in-workspace edits support different moments in the job.
  • Escape key consistency — Escape closes overlays, drawers, and the chat panel consistently across the product, matching user expectations from desktop applications.

Recommended Next Steps

Prioritized by user impact and clarity of design intent. Ship honest states before new features.

  1. Align every visible control with a real outcome
    Global save/share/settings, export actions, and canvas add flows should either work or read as unavailable — never silent. Update the pattern library and component specs accordingly (NF-1–NF-5).
  2. Pair labels with fields and required rules
    Document and apply the same label–field pattern everywhere; make “required” mean the same thing in behavior and in assistive tech (A-1, W-2).
  3. Name every icon-only control
    Activity rail, zoom, theme, and close controls need short names that match what sighted users see in tooltips (A-3, A-6).
  4. Specify selection patterns for radio and multi-select
    Single-select and multi-select should look and behave consistently, including keyboard and screen reader behavior (A-2).
  5. Design for session recovery
    Autosave, resume prompts, and clear copy when session data cannot be restored (W-1).
  6. Bring the configuration overlay up to the modal standard
    Focus stays inside until closed; focus returns to the opener; overlay and close are named (A-4).
  7. Validate on Continue; distinguish Skip
    Flow spec: what Continue enforces vs. what Skip defers (W-2).
  8. Protect Finalize with confirmation and visible locked state
    Banner, disabled fields, and clear unlock path (D-1).
  9. Document workspace tabs and layer tabs as one tab pattern
    Same keyboard model and selection announcement everywhere (A-5).
  10. Resolve duplicate sub-steps and “None” conflicts
    Information architecture and content design for local code and mechanical groups; mutual exclusion for heating and features (W-3, W-4, W-5).
